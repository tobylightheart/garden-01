What is a digital garden?

planted · 2026-06-02 tended · 2026-06-02 stage · evergreen

A garden is a place, not a stream.

A blog is a stream. Posts arrive in order, the newest on top, each one finished and frozen the moment it's published. To read a blog is to stand at the mouth of a river and watch the water go by.

A digital garden is a place you walk through instead. Notes aren't sorted by date; they're sorted by how they connect. You enter wherever you like and follow paths between ideas. Nothing is finished, because in a garden nothing is ever finished — there's only what's in season, what's been pruned lately, and what's gone to seed.

You don't subscribe to a garden. You visit it, and notice what's changed since last time.

What makes it a garden

  • Notes grow. A page can be edited forever. Its date isn't when it was "published" but when it was last tended.
  • Notes link. Density of connection matters more than recency. Ideas earn their place by what they touch.
  • Notes are honest about ripeness. Each one says whether it's a 🌱 seedling, 🌿 budding, or 🌳 evergreen — so you know how much weight to put on it.
  • Imperfection is the point. Publishing a rough thought is a feature. It invites tending.

Why bother

The feed trained us to only share things that are finished and flattering. That's a high bar, and it kills a lot of thinking before it starts. A garden lowers the bar back down to the ground: plant the half-thought, let it sit, come back when you know more. Related: tending, not publishing and on slowness.

This little plot is itself an example. Four notes, one stylesheet, no database, no build step. See the colophon if you want to know how it's wired.
