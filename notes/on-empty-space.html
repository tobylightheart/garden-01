On empty space

planted · 2026-06-02 tended · 2026-06-02 stage · seedling

The unplanted parts of a garden aren't waste. They're what makes it a garden and not a field.

I notice I want to fill things. Given a blank page, the easy instinct is to cover it — more words, more links, more notes in the bed until the margin is gone. But a garden you've planted edge to edge isn't lush. It's just crowded. You can't walk through it.

The empty space is the path. It's the breath between two thoughts. On a page, it's the wide margin that tells your eye where to rest; in a garden, it's the patch of bare soil that says something could go here, but not yet, and maybe not ever, and that's fine.

A few things I keep relearning about leaving room:

  • White space isn't absence. It's the frame that makes the planted thing legible at all.
  • An unfinished note is mostly empty space, and that emptiness is an invitation — to me later, or to whoever inherits the plot.
  • The hardest edit is the one that removes rather than adds. Subtraction feels like loss until you see what the gap lets breathe.
You don't decorate silence. You let it be the thing the music happens inside of.

Maybe this is just slowness wearing a different coat — both are really about refusing to fill every available moment or pixel. A slow page and a spacious one are the same kindness aimed at different senses. And like every note in this garden, this one is deliberately mostly margin: a seedling with room left over to grow into.
