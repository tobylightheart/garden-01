Tending, not publishing

planted · 2026-06-02 tended · 2026-06-02 stage · budding

The verb you choose changes what you make.

Publishing is a one-shot act. You polish, you press the button, and the thing is launched into the world fully formed, never to be touched again. The whole posture is defensive: get it right, because you only get to do it once.

Tending is a habit. You come back to the same patch over and over. You pull a weed, move a sentence into the light, notice that two notes are really one. The posture is relaxed, because nothing is final and everything can be fixed next time you pass by.

What tending feels like in practice

  • Writing the rough version today instead of the perfect version never.
  • Letting a note say "I'm not sure about this yet" out loud, with a 🌱 seedling tag.
  • Editing old notes without apology or changelog — the garden simply looks different than it did.
  • Treating links as the real work: a note becomes useful when it connects to others.

A small tending log

Because tending is the point, it's worth recording. This note's own log:

  • 2026-06-02 — planted as a seedling; one paragraph and a title.
  • 2026-06-02 — promoted to budding; added the practice list and this log.
A finished thing asks to be admired. A tended thing asks to be visited again.

This idea is the working philosophy behind the whole garden, and it pairs naturally with slowness.
